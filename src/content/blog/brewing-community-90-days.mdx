Cognition built the first AI software engineer. That alone changes how people write code. But a product this consequential doesn't reach its potential through feature releases and documentation pages. It reaches it through a community that teaches the world how to use it.

Right now, Cognition is early in that work. Cursor already runs events in over 200 cities. They have ambassadors, a content machine, and a recognizable grassroots identity built around coffee shops and local builders. Cognition has Devin, a Discord, and a Luma calendar. That's a starting position, not a moat.

This post is the strategy I'd execute in the first 90 days of building Cognition's developer community. Three programs, sequenced to build on each other, designed to create a self-sustaining engine rather than a series of one-off gestures.

<ProsePullQuote>
The best developer communities aren't audiences. They're operating systems, with curriculum, local chapters, and a feedback loop that makes the product better.
</ProsePullQuote>

## The case for starting now

Devin is a paradigm shift. It's not a copilot that autocompletes your next line. It's an autonomous engineer you direct at problems. That requires a different mental model, and right now, most developers are still learning it through trial and error.

Cursor understood early that community accelerates adoption. Their Cafe Cursor format (take over a coffee shop, bring credits and good energy, let people build together) created warmth at scale. But warmth without structure doesn't compound. Events end. Attendees scatter. The energy dissipates until the next one.

What Cognition needs is a community engine: curriculum that creates competence, chapters that create belonging, and ambassadors that create reach. Sequenced properly, each one feeds the next.

## The 90-day plan

### Days 0–30: The House Roast, curriculum and certification

The first priority is giving people a structured path from "curious about Devin" to "confident Devin orchestrator." Three tracks:

- **Devin 101**: the first agent workflow, the orchestrator mindset, PR review basics. The on-ramp.
- **Advanced Workflows**: multi-repo orchestration, Devin fleets, MCP integrations, playbook authoring, the non-coding use cases most tutorials skip.
- **Devin for Teams**: rollout patterns, knowledge onboarding, enterprise governance. The content that sells seats.

Each track ends with an earnable, verifiable certification badge. Developers love credentials: they signal competence to hiring managers and give community members something to work toward.

<ProsePullQuote>
Nobody has formal Devin certification yet. Cursor has great educational content but no credential. This is greenfield.
</ProsePullQuote>

The curriculum isn't just courses. It's community-contributed playbooks, hands-on labs, and a heavy emphasis on the underserved non-coding use cases (research synthesis, living documentation, design-system maintenance, GTM automation) that widen the funnel beyond senior engineers.

### Days 30–60: Single-Origin Series, Cafe Cognition

With a curriculum in place, the next step is getting people together. Cafe Cognition is the meetup format: pop-ups in coffee shops, Devin credits, live demos, and local builders sharing what they've built.

But the difference from a one-off event series is the **meetup-in-a-box kit**, everything a host needs in a single package: run-of-show, slide deck, live-demo script, swag templates, and a photo checklist. Reduce friction to the point where a motivated developer in Bangalore or Berlin can run a Cafe Cognition with zero coordination overhead.

Pilot in SF and NYC first. Measure repeat attendance, builds shipped, and host satisfaction, not just headcount.

<ProseWaxFigure caption="The meetup-in-a-box kit">
- **Run-of-show template**: minute-by-minute schedule that works for 15 or 50 people
- **Slide deck**: branded, editable, with speaker notes
- **Live-demo script**: a working Devin workflow hosts can present or riff on
- **Swag templates**: sticker sheets, name badges, table cards
- **Photo checklist**: the 5 shots that make great social proof
- **Host leaderboard**: recognition and perks that turn great organizers into a flywheel
</ProseWaxFigure>

### Days 60–90: The Roasters' Guild, the ambassador program

The curriculum creates practitioners. The meetups create local energy. The ambassador program turns the most active community members into a sustained force multiplier.

Three tiers, each with clear expectations and rewards:

- **Contributor**: entry tier. Share builds publicly, host a first event, get early access to features and beta programs.
- **Advocate**: consistent organizers and content creators. Co-branded workshops, swag drops, and a direct line to the DevRel team.
- **Champion**: regional leaders. A product-feedback seat at the table, flagship event budget, and recognition that matters on a resume.

The critical piece is the feedback loop: a weekly Devin Office Hours session where ambassadors surface real user friction, which flows into community playbooks and back to the product team. This isn't a megaphone. It's a two-way channel.
