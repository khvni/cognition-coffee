When I was fourteen, I told a friend that my replacement for social media was writing answers on Quora. He said it was the stupidest thing he had ever heard. Then he went home, opened Quora, and wrote for four hours straight.

Years later he wrote [a whole essay](https://press.alif.build/p/what-writing-on-quora-taught-me-about) about that night. He called me the weird friend. He is not wrong.

His essay tells his side. This is mine, and it starts earlier, because by the time I talked him into it I was already hooked. I wasn't recommending a hobby. I was handing him the thing I couldn't put down: a number that told me the truth about my own writing.

## The number that wouldn't lie

I was not the kid who kept a journal or stayed after class to talk to the English teacher. I came home, did not do my homework, and wrote answers until two in the morning. My parents were, reasonably, concerned. I'd publish something, hate it by midnight, delete it, and write it again from scratch. Then I'd refresh the page to watch the view count move.

That count was the whole point. School grades came filtered through a teacher and a rubric. Likes on a feed were a popularity contest with my friends. Quora was neither. If an answer was good, strangers who owed me nothing read it to the end and shared it. If it was bad, it died at two hundred views and stayed there. No algorithm to game, no social debt to call in. Just one question, asked over and over: does this land?

Some nights it landed hard. A throwaway answer about parental advice climbed past fifty thousand views in a week. The next one, which I was sure was sharper, stalled and never moved. At first I had no idea why. Then the pattern started to show itself. I could see what made someone stop scrolling, what made them send it to a friend, where exactly they lost interest and left. By the time I was fifteen I had written answers that reached millions of people. The verified number I still quote, six million views, came from a teenager skipping homework to chase a refresh button.

<ProsePullQuote cite="the lesson, learned twice">
You cannot tell yourself you are good at something. Other people have to tell you.
</ProsePullQuote>

That is the line my friend landed on in his essay, and it is the one I would put on the wall. For a year I competed with adults who had been paid to write for decades, and the only scoreboard that mattered was whether real people kept reading. You can believe your own work is brilliant. I did, constantly. The belief is worthless until someone who isn't you confirms it.

## Why a view counter is the whole job

I do not write on Quora anymore. I build communities. From the outside those look like different things. They are the same thing.

A community is a view counter you cannot fake. You can tell yourself the curriculum is great, the event was a hit, the tool is obviously worth adopting. None of that is true until the people you built it for tell you it is. They tell you by showing up again, by bringing a friend, by shipping something and posting it without being asked. That is the only honest signal, and learning to read it at fourteen is the most useful thing I ever did by accident.

It is also the entire job of developer relations, stated plainly. Marketing copy is me telling myself my writing is good. A developer who tries the tool, gets unstuck, and comes back is the view count moving. You cannot argue a community into believing a product is good any more than I could argue a stranger into finishing a bad answer. You earn the signal or you don't get it.

When I scaled a tech community from one campus club to more than thirty chapters, I wasn't running a marketing plan. I was watching the counter. Which chapters kept meeting after the founder graduated. Which events people drove across a city to attend. Which formats died quietly at two hundred views. Same instinct, bigger room.

## The obsession came before the plan

My friend ends his essay with a line I keep coming back to: the obsession came before the plan. He's right about that too. Neither of us was being strategic at fourteen. I just could not stop refreshing the page.

But the instinct that obsession trained is the one I would bring to building Cognition's developer community. Not telling people Devin is good. Building the place where they tell each other, where the signal is loud and honest, and where the community itself is the proof. The best version of that job is a view counter for an entire ecosystem, and I have been reading one since I was fourteen.
